Simple Recipes

Lavender Rosemary Candle

Candle • Relaxing • Beginner

A soft herbal candle with a calming scent profile. Dried herbs look beautiful, but scent usually comes best from essential oil or candle fragrance.

  • Soy wax or beeswax
  • 1 wick
  • Lavender essential oil or candle fragrance
  • A little dried lavender or rosemary for decoration
  • Heat-safe jar
  1. Melt the wax gently.
  2. Add fragrance or essential oil after removing from high heat.
  3. Pour into the jar and center the wick.
  4. Add only a very small amount of dried herbs, away from the wick.
  5. Let cool fully before trimming the wick.

Calendula Soap

Soap • Gentle • Herbal

Calendula is a classic soap herb because the petals are beautiful and infused calendula oil is often used in gentle skincare-style bars.

  • Melt-and-pour soap base or prepared soap base
  • Calendula infused oil or dried calendula petals
  • Soap mold
  • Optional gentle scent
  1. Melt the soap base slowly.
  2. Stir in a small amount of calendula oil or dried petals.
  3. Pour into the mold.
  4. Let it harden fully before removing.

Lavender Mint Sugar Scrub

Scrub • Fast • Great gift

This is one of the easiest body-care recipes to make. Lavender gives a relaxing note, while mint adds a fresh cooling feel.

  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1/2 cup carrier oil such as almond, olive, or coconut
  • Lavender essential oil
  • Mint or peppermint essential oil
  • Jar with lid
  1. Mix the sugar and oil.
  2. Add a few drops of lavender and mint oils.
  3. Stir until it feels soft and scoopable.
  4. Store in a jar and use carefully, since oils can make the shower slippery.

Gentle Garden Salve (Idea)

Calendula • Yarrow • Lavender • Chamomile

This is a simple pattern, not a full medical formula: use flower‑infused oil and a little beeswax to make a soft salve for dry hands and rough spots.

  • Herb‑infused oil made with gentle flowers (such as calendula, lavender, chamomile, or yarrow)
  • Beeswax pellets or shaved beeswax
  • Small jars or tins
  1. Warm the infused oil gently in a heat‑safe container.
  2. Stir in a small amount of beeswax until melted, then test a spoonful in the fridge to see how firm it becomes.
  3. Adjust with a little more oil (softer) or beeswax (firmer) until you like the texture.
  4. Pour into clean jars or tins, let cool, and label with herbs and date.

Simple Flower Body Oil

Calendula • Chamomile • Rose • Lavender

Use dried garden flowers to make a gentle body oil you can use on hands, arms, or legs. This same method also works with hibiscus, yarrow, and lemon balm.

  • Clean, dried petals or flowers (such as calendula, chamomile, rose, or lavender)
  • A mild carrier oil (such as olive, sunflower, or sweet almond)
  • Clean glass jar with lid
  1. Fill the jar halfway with dried flowers.
  2. Cover the flowers completely with oil, leaving a little space at the top.
  3. Close the jar and let it sit in a warm, shaded spot for 2–4 weeks, shaking gently now and then.
  4. Strain out the flowers through a cloth, and store the finished oil in a clean jar.
  5. Label the jar with the herbs and date, and use within a few months, watching for any off smell.

Recipe note

📝 For candles, dried botanicals are mostly decorative and should be used sparingly. For stronger scent, fragrance oils or essential oils usually work better than whole herbs.

Simple Whipped Body Butter

Recipe • Skin care • Beginner friendly

  • ½ cup shea butter — the base; deeply moisturizing and solid at room temperature.
  • ¼ cup coconut oil — melts into skin quickly and adds a light, non-greasy feel.
  • 2 tablespoons sweet almond or jojoba oil — keeps the texture silky and helps it spread easily.
  • 20–30 drops essential oil of your choice — lavender, vanilla, or rose are popular options.
  • Melt shea butter and coconut oil together, then let cool in the fridge for 30 minutes until semi-solid. Add the carrier oil and essential oils, then whip with a hand mixer for 3–5 minutes until fluffy. Store in a glass jar at room temperature.

Herb-Infused Body Butter

Recipe • Skin care • Uses your garden herbs

  • ½ cup herb-infused oil — made by steeping dried lavender, calendula, or chamomile in olive or sunflower oil for 2–4 weeks in a cool, dark place (or 2–3 hours in a slow cooker on low).
  • ½ cup shea butter — whips up creamy and absorbs well without feeling heavy.
  • 1 tablespoon beeswax pellets (optional) — adds a firmer texture and a slight protective barrier on the skin.
  • Melt shea butter and beeswax (if using) together. Stir in the infused oil and let cool until semi-solid. Whip until fluffy. The herb oil gives this a soft, natural scent without added essential oils.

Minty Cooling Body Butter

Recipe • Skin care • Great for sore muscles

  • ½ cup shea butter
  • ¼ cup coconut oil
  • 2 tablespoons argan or jojoba oil — lightweight and fast-absorbing.
  • 20 drops peppermint essential oil + 10 drops eucalyptus essential oil — creates a cooling, tingly sensation that feels great after workouts or on tired feet.
  • Melt, cool, whip as in the Simple recipe. Avoid use on broken skin or near the eyes. Keep away from children under 6 due to the peppermint concentration.

Infused Oils

What is an infused oil?

Calendula • Lavender • Rosemary

An infused oil is a carrier oil that slowly pulls helpful plant properties from dried herbs or flowers. These oils can be used in soaps, salves, and body care.

Basic cold infusion

Slow method • Best for storage

  1. Use fully dried herbs or petals only.
  2. Fill a clean dry jar about halfway with herbs.
  3. Pour in olive, sunflower, or sweet almond oil until the herbs are fully covered.
  4. Seal the jar and keep it in a warm dark place for 4 to 6 weeks.
  5. Shake gently every day or two.
  6. Strain well and store in a clean bottle.

Quick warm infusion

Faster method • Gentle heat

  1. Add dried herbs and oil to a heat-safe jar or double boiler.
  2. Warm very gently for 2 to 4 hours.
  3. Do not boil or fry the herbs.
  4. Cool, strain, and bottle.

Important safety note

Fresh herbs can introduce water into oil and may cause mold or spoilage if stored. Dried herbs are the safer choice for oils you plan to keep.

Oil patterns you can reuse

Most recipes for oils follow the same simple pattern: dried herbs + mild oil + time. You can often swap in any gentle, clean, dried herb or flower from your garden.

  • Good for simple body oils: calendula, chamomile, lavender, lemon balm, rose, yarrow.
  • Nice in fresh‑feeling blends: mint, lemongrass, basil (always dried well first).
  • Use clean, unsprayed plants and dry them completely before putting them into oil.

Skin safety note

For body products, pick herbs and flowers from clean plants (no sprays), dry them well, and test new recipes on a small patch of skin first.

Skin soothing • Salves • Oils

Mint is often used in refreshing body products like foot scrubs, bath salts, and cooling soap blends. It is best used thoughtfully because strong mint oils can irritate skin.

Lemon balm

Gentle • Calming • Herbal care

Salve patterns you can reuse

Most simple salves follow the same pattern: herb‑infused oil + a little beeswax. You can often switch the flowers or herbs as long as they are gentle and dried well.

  • Basic pattern: make an infused oil first, then gently melt in beeswax until it sets softly.

Garden Residents

Golden Pothos

Indoors • Low to indirect light • Low water

  • Light: thrives in low to medium indirect light — the more light it gets, the more golden variegation shows on the leaves. Avoid harsh direct sun which scorches the leaves.
  • Watering: water when the top inch of soil is dry — roughly every 7–10 days in summer, every 14 days in winter. Always let it drain fully; never let it sit in standing water.
  • Outdoors: can go outside in a shaded or dappled-light spot in spring and summer — bring back inside before first frost.
  • Yellow leaves = overwatering or too little light. Let soil dry out more between waterings and move to a brighter spot.
  • Brown crispy tips = too dry or low humidity. Mist the leaves lightly or set the pot on a tray of pebbles with water.
  • Pests: mealybugs or spider mites occasionally appear — wipe leaves with a damp cloth dipped in diluted neem oil or rubbing alcohol.
  • Not used in candles, soaps, or skin products — mildly toxic to dogs if chewed, so keep it out of reach.

Wax Plant (Hoya)

Indoors • Bright indirect light • Low water

  • Light: needs bright indirect light to bloom — an east or west-facing window is ideal. Low light keeps it alive but it will rarely flower.
  • Watering: water every 10–14 days in summer, every 3–4 weeks in winter. Let the soil dry out completely between waterings — soggy roots cause rot quickly.
  • Blooming tip: do not remove the little brown flower spurs after blooms fall — new flowers grow from the same spur each season.
  • Outdoors: can spend summer outside in a shaded spot — benefits from the humidity but needs protection from direct sun and temperatures below 50°F.
  • Wrinkled leaves = underwatering. Give it a thorough soak and the leaves will plump back up within a day or two.
  • Pests: mealybugs are the most common issue — dab them with a cotton swab dipped in rubbing alcohol, or spray with diluted neem oil.
  • The flowers have a naturally sweet, honey-like scent — lovely inspiration for candle and soap fragrance ideas. Pet-safe and non-toxic to dogs.

Cilantro

Outdoors • Cool season • Full sun to partial shade • Regular water

  • Timing: plant in San Antonio during fall (September–November) or late winter (February–March). It bolts and turns bitter in summer heat — do not try to grow it in June, July, or August.
  • Light: full sun to partial shade — morning sun with afternoon shade extends the harvest window before bolting.
  • Watering: keep soil consistently moist but not soggy — water every 2–3 days or when the top inch feels dry. Letting it dry out speeds up bolting.
  • Harvest: snip leaves from the outer stems first, never more than one-third of the plant at a time. Regular harvesting delays bolting.
  • Bolting (flowering early) = usually caused by heat or inconsistent watering. Once it bolts, let the seeds dry on the plant — collect them as coriander seed for cooking or replanting.
  • Pests: aphids occasionally cluster on young stems — blast them off with water or spray with diluted neem oil.
  • Fresh leaves and dried seeds (coriander) have a bright citrusy scent occasionally used in natural soaps. Safe for dogs.

Roma Tomatoes

Outdoors • Full sun • Regular deep water

  • Timing: plant transplants after last frost (mid-March in San Antonio). Roma tomatoes handle heat better than many varieties but still produce best in spring before peak summer temperatures.
  • Watering: water deeply 2–3 times per week — tomatoes need consistent moisture to prevent blossom end rot. Mulch heavily around the base to retain soil moisture.
  • Feeding: fertilize every 2 weeks with a tomato-specific fertilizer once flowers appear. Too much nitrogen early on produces leaves instead of fruit.
  • Blossom end rot (black bottom on fruit) = inconsistent watering or calcium deficiency. Water more consistently and add crushed eggshells or a calcium spray around the base.
  • Yellowing lower leaves = normal as the plant matures, but widespread yellowing = overwatering or nitrogen deficiency. Adjust watering and add fertilizer.
  • Pests: hornworms, aphids, and whiteflies are common in San Antonio. Handpick hornworms; use neem oil spray for aphids and whiteflies. Plant marigolds nearby as a natural deterrent.
  • Not used in candles or soaps, but tomatoes attract pollinators that help all surrounding garden plants thrive.

Bougainvillea (Bugambilia)

Outdoors • Full sun • Low water once established

  • Watering: water deeply once a week while establishing (first season). Once established, water only every 2–3 weeks — it actually blooms better when slightly stressed by drought.
  • Pruning: prune after each bloom cycle to encourage the next flush of color. Cut back about one-third of the stems — it responds very well to pruning.
  • Feeding: fertilize every 4–6 weeks during growing season with a high-phosphorus fertilizer to encourage blooming. Too much nitrogen = lots of leaves, few bracts.
  • Frost protection: bougainvillea can handle light frosts but needs covering or moving to a sheltered spot if temps drop below 28°F. It may lose leaves but usually bounces back in spring.
  • Not blooming = usually needs more sun or a dry stress period. Move to a sunnier spot and cut back watering for 3–4 weeks to trigger a new bloom cycle.
  • Craft use: dried bracts can be used as decorative botanicals in candles — dry completely before use. Not for skin products.

Jade Plant

Indoors or outdoors • Bright light • Very low water

  • Light indoors: place near the brightest window in the house — a south or west-facing window is ideal. Rotate the pot every few weeks so all sides get equal light.
  • Light outdoors: can go outside in spring and summer in a spot with morning sun and afternoon shade — direct afternoon sun in San Antonio can scorch the leaves.
  • Watering: water thoroughly every 2–3 weeks in summer, every 4–6 weeks in winter. Always let the soil dry out completely before watering again — overwatering is by far the most common cause of jade plant death.
  • Mushy stems or dropping leaves = root rot from overwatering. Remove from pot, trim any black or mushy roots, let dry for 2–3 days, then repot in fresh cactus mix.
  • Wrinkled or shriveled leaves = underwatering. Give it a thorough soak and leaves should plump back up within a few days.
  • Pests: mealybugs and scale insects are the most common — remove with a cotton swab dipped in rubbing alcohol, or treat with neem oil spray.
  • Not used in candles, soaps, or skin products — mildly toxic if ingested; keep away from dogs and small children.

ZZ Plant (Zamioculcas)

Indoors • Low to indirect light • Very low water

  • Light: tolerates low light better than almost any other houseplant — even a dim hallway or office corner works. Brighter indirect light encourages faster growth but is not required.
  • Watering: water thoroughly only when the soil is completely dry — roughly every 3–4 weeks in summer, every 6–8 weeks in winter. It stores water in thick underground rhizomes and will rot if overwatered.
  • Yellow leaves = almost always overwatering. Let the soil dry out completely and hold off watering for at least 4 weeks.
  • The purplish color on new stems is completely normal — not a sign of illness or stress.
  • Dusty leaves reduce light absorption — wipe the leaves gently with a damp cloth every few weeks to keep them clean and shiny.
  • Pests: rarely affected but scale or mealybugs can appear — treat with neem oil spray or rubbing alcohol on a cotton swab.
  • Not used in candles, soaps, or skin products — toxic to dogs if chewed, so keep it out of reach.

Orchid

Indoors • Bright indirect light • Low water

  • Light: bright indirect light is essential — an east-facing windowsill is ideal. A sheer curtain over a south or west window also works well. Direct sun burns the leaves.
  • Watering: water once a week by running water over the roots for 15–20 seconds, then letting it drain completely. Never let roots sit in standing water — this causes root rot fast.
  • Humidity: orchids love humidity — mist lightly every few days or set the pot on a tray of pebbles with water underneath (but not touching the pot bottom).
  • After blooms fall: do not discard — cut the stem just above the lowest node (bump on the stem). It will often produce a new bloom spike within 2–4 months.
  • Yellow leaves = too much direct sun or overwatering. Wrinkled leaves = underwatering or low humidity. Mushy black roots = root rot — trim with sterile scissors and repot in fresh orchid bark.
  • Pests: scale, mealybugs, and fungus gnats are the most common. Treat with neem oil spray or rubbing alcohol. Fungus gnats = soil is staying too wet between waterings.
  • Dried orchid petals can be used as delicate candle decoration — dry completely first. Non-toxic and safe for dogs.

Money Tree (Pachira)

Indoors • Bright indirect light • Moderate water

  • Light: bright indirect light is ideal — a spot near a window that gets morning sun works well. Once you find a good spot, leave it there; money trees do not like being moved and may drop leaves in protest.
  • Watering: water thoroughly when the top 2 inches of soil are dry — roughly every 1–2 weeks in summer, every 2–3 weeks in winter. Always let it drain completely; standing water in the saucer causes root rot.
  • Outdoors: can go outside in a shaded or dappled-light spot during warm months — bring back inside before temperatures drop below 50°F.
  • Yellow leaves = overwatering or too little light. Let the soil dry out more and move to a brighter spot.
  • Leaf drop = usually caused by moving the plant, cold drafts, or a sudden change in temperature or humidity. Keep away from AC vents and cold windows in winter.
  • Pests: scale, spider mites, and mealybugs occasionally appear — treat with neem oil spray or wipe leaves with a cloth dampened with diluted rubbing alcohol.
  • Traditionally associated with good luck and positive energy. Non-toxic and safe for dogs.

Heartleaf Philodendron

Indoors • Low to indirect light • Moderate water

  • Light: tolerates low to medium indirect light very well — one of the best plants for less-bright rooms. Brighter indirect light = faster growth and larger leaves. Avoid direct sun which scorches the leaves.
  • Watering: water when the top inch of soil feels dry — roughly every 7–10 days in summer, every 10–14 days in winter. It tolerates some drought but grows best with consistent moisture.
  • Moss pole care: mist the moss pole regularly to keep it moist — the aerial roots will attach to a damp pole much more readily than a dry one.
  • Outdoors: can go outside in a fully shaded spot during warm months — it burns easily in direct sun. Bring back inside before temperatures drop below 55°F.
  • Yellow leaves = overwatering. Brown crispy leaf edges = low humidity or underwatering. Leggy, sparse growth = not enough light — move to a brighter spot.
  • Pests: mealybugs, spider mites, and fungus gnats are the most common. Treat mealybugs and mites with neem oil spray; fungus gnats = let soil dry out more between waterings.
  • Not used in candles, soaps, or skin products — mildly toxic to dogs if chewed, so keep it out of reach.

Growing in 78253

Heat‑tough backbone plants

Full sun • Low to moderate water

  • Rosemary – very reliable in hot, sunny beds or large containers when soil drains well.
  • Sage – strong choice for San Antonio heat; likes full sun and good drainage.
  • Thyme – small but tough; great in raised beds, borders, or containers with sharp drainage.
  • Oregano – hardy and easy in warm, sunny spaces; pairs well with other Mediterranean herbs.
  • Aloe Vera – thrives in San Antonio heat with almost no effort; water only when soil is completely dry.
  • Echinacea – tough perennial that comes back every year; plant once and it self-seeds and spreads over time.

Heat lovers & showy flowers

Full sun • Great for color

  • Lemongrass – thrives in hot South Texas weather; good in beds or large pots.
  • Hibiscus – loves heat and sun; can be a colorful shrub or large container plant.
  • Marigold – direct sow from seed after last frost; blooms all season and also repels garden pests naturally.
  • Jasmine – a vining plant that needs a fence or trellis; blooms in spring with incredibly fragrant white flowers.
  • Yarrow – hardy flowering herb that does well in hot, sunny beds once established.
  • Cornflower – pretty blue flower for cooler or milder parts of the season; prefers full sun.

Good with a little extra care

Gentler conditions • Containers help

  • Lavender – likes full sun but needs excellent drainage; raised beds or pots are best.
  • Lemon balm – prefers morning sun and afternoon shade; appreciates protection from peak heat.
  • Chamomile – better in the cooler season; may tire out faster in strong summer heat.
  • Cornflower – also enjoys milder weather; treat as a cool‑season flower in your area.

Container‑friendly plants

Great for patios & control

  • Peppermint – must be kept in a container; spreads aggressively underground if planted in a bed.
  • Spearmint – same rule as peppermint; container only, with morning sun and afternoon shade.
  • Mint – grows very well but spreads; best kept in its own pot.
  • Aloe Vera – thrives in a sandy, well-draining pot on a sunny porch or windowsill.
  • Lemon balm – gentle plant that does nicely in containers with regular water.
  • Basil – loves warm weather; containers make it easy to keep watered and harvested.
  • Rosemary or sage – also happy in large pots with sunny exposure and good drainage.

Trees & large container plants

Bring indoors during hard freezes

  • Meyer Lemon Tree – grow in a 15-gallon container in full sun; move inside if a hard freeze is expected. Water when the top 2 inches of soil are dry and fertilize with citrus fertilizer in spring and summer.
  • Avocado (Mexicola variety) – the most cold-hardy option for San Antonio; grow in a large container so it can be protected during hard freezes. Water deeply and let soil dry slightly between waterings.
  • Jasmine (Confederate) – plant near a fence or trellis; once established it is very drought tolerant and fills the yard with fragrance every spring.

Vegetables for soaps & skin care

Edible and useful in the craft garden

  • Carrot (cool season) – plant September–October or February–March; direct sow in loose, deep soil at least 12 inches deep. Purée adds natural golden color to handmade soaps.
  • Cucumber (warm season) – plant after last frost (mid-March); water frequently and mulch well. Fresh purée or juice adds a cooling, moisturizing effect to melt-and-pour soaps.

Simple timing guide

For a typical 78253 backyard

  • Spring (Mar–May): basil, chamomile, calendula, cornflower, lemon balm, marigold, cucumber — plant after last frost.
  • Summer (Jun–Aug): lemongrass, hibiscus, rosemary, sage, thyme, oregano, mint in pots, aloe vera.
  • Fall (Sep–Nov): great time to plant perennial herbs — rosemary, sage, oregano, thyme, echinacea, yarrow, lemon balm, jasmine. Also sow carrots in September–October.
  • Cool months (Nov–Feb): best window for chamomile, calendula, and cornflower. Sow carrots again in February–March.
  • Year-round indoors: aloe vera, money tree, heartleaf philodendron, lemon tree (container), avocado (container).

Backyard labels & safety

What the little notes mean

  • Heat‑tough = Does well in hot, sunny San Antonio weather once established.
  • Container‑friendly = Happy in pots; easier to water, move, and keep under control.
  • Cool‑season = Prefers gentler weather; plant in cooler months instead of peak summer.
  • Bring inside = Needs frost protection; grow in a container so it can be moved indoors during hard freezes.
  • Clean plants only = Use herbs and flowers that are not sprayed with chemicals if they will touch skin.
